Narakorn Ruangjam

Student ID: 2411220227
Microstructure & Mechanical Properties

Led material characterization — comparing the microstructure and mechanical properties (tensile strength, yield, modulus, density) of Aluminum 6061-T6, CFRP, and PA12 Nylon. Built the material property comparison table that anchors the selection decision.

Before this project I treated material properties as numbers in a table. Working on the microstructure analysis taught me why those numbers exist — that Aluminium 6061-T6's strength comes from fine Mg₂Si precipitates, and that PA12's porosity is what makes it fail early. I learned to read a datasheet as a story about structure, not just a list of values.

The hardest part was connecting microstructure back to the FEA results the team produced. Reconciling the "why" (microstructure) with the "what" (stress numbers) forced me to keep questioning my own assumptions until the whole picture was consistent.

FMEA & Modular Mechanism Design

Designed the modular bayonet finger interface and ran the Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA) — identifying primary failure modes at the pivot joints and lug roots, then feeding mitigation requirements back into the mechanism design.

Designing the modular bayonet interface and running the FMEA changed how I approach design — from "will it work?" to "how will it fail?". Listing failure modes and scoring their risk made me add the lug-root fillet and the detent deliberately, instead of by habit.

I had to revise the mechanism several times as new failure modes surfaced. Staying flexible and letting go of my first design, rather than defending it, was the real lesson of this project.

Critical & Analytical Thinking Resilience, Flexibility & Agility
RM

Rujinarong Meeda

Student ID: 2411290014
Structural Analysis & Validation

Led the AI-assisted computational structural analysis across all three materials under identical 50 N grip and 15 N side loads — deriving Von Mises stress, displacement, and factor of safety from certified datasheet material properties to validate the design.

Working through the AI-assisted structural analysis taught me that results are only as good as the boundary conditions and material inputs you define. Setting up identical loads and fixtures across three materials so the comparison would be fair turned out to be harder than running the analysis itself.

When early results looked off, I learned to question my own boundary conditions, fixtures, and units rather than accept the first answer. That habit of distrusting a clean-looking result until I understand why it came out that way is what I'll carry forward.

Course Context

ENG-203 Engineering Materials

This project was completed as part of ENG-203 at the Engineering Department. The course focuses on engineering design, materials selection, and lean manufacturing principles applied to real-world automation challenges.

The gripper project was chosen to bridge mechanical design, material science, and manufacturing process knowledge within a single integrated deliverable.

Project Scope

What We Built

A modular two-finger gripper designed for lean automation. The modularity allows finger profiles to be swapped without dismantling the actuator assembly, reducing changeover time in multi-variant production lines.

Three materials — Aluminum 6061-T6, CFRP, and SLS PA12 Nylon — were evaluated through FEA simulation and physical load testing to identify the optimal candidate for each use-case scenario.
