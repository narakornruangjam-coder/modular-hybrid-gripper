1. Introduction

Robotic grippers are a critical component in flexible manufacturing systems. The ability to rapidly reconfigure a gripper for different part geometries is central to lean automation, where small batch sizes and high product variety demand frequent changeover. This project develops and evaluates a modular two-finger gripper architecture that decouples the actuator body from the interchangeable finger elements.

Three structural materials are compared: Aluminum 6061-T6, Carbon Fibre Reinforced Polymer (CFRP), and Selective Laser Sintered (SLS) PA12 Nylon. The comparison is framed around structural performance under grip load, weight, and total cost of ownership at small production volumes.

2. Objectives

  • Design a two-finger gripper with a tool-free modular finger interface compatible with UR5-series robots.
  • Evaluate Aluminum 6061-T6, CFRP, and SLS PA12 Nylon under identical static loading conditions using FEA.
  • Validate simulation results for the two prototype-accessible materials (Al 6061 and PA12) through physical load testing.
  • Recommend the optimal material for production use based on structural performance, weight, and cost.

3. Design

The gripper consists of a fixed actuator body housing a pneumatic cylinder and a tool-free bayonet interface into which standardised finger elements lock. Each finger is a single-part component with a V-groove contact face and a bayonet base that engages with four radial lugs on the actuator.

The finger is designed to be manufactured in any of the three candidate materials without changes to the interface geometry. This ensures dimensional interchangeability and allows the FEA comparison to isolate material effects.

5. Microstructure

Mechanical behaviour is rooted in microstructure. Aluminum 6061-T6 is a wrought, precipitation-hardened alloy: the T6 temper disperses fine coherent Mg₂Si precipitates through equiaxed grains, pinning dislocations and giving a largely isotropic, predictable response. CFRP consists of continuous carbon fibres in a polymer matrix (~60% fibre volume), so stiffness and strength are highly anisotropic and depend on fibre–matrix adhesion. SLS PA12 is a semi-crystalline polyamide built by fusing powder; incomplete particle coalescence leaves ~3–5% residual porosity and partially-melted boundaries that act as crack-initiation sites.

This explains the structural ranking: the aluminium's precipitate-strengthened grains and CFRP's aligned fibres carry the grip load efficiently, while PA12's porous sintered structure concentrates stress and fails early.

7. Finite Element Analysis

Static structural FEA was conducted in SolidWorks Simulation 2023. Each finger model used a curvature-based solid mesh with a global element size of 2 mm and local refinement to 0.5 mm at stress concentrations (lug roots and groove radius).

7.1 Boundary Conditions

The bayonet base face was fully constrained (fixed support). A 50 N distributed load was applied to the V-groove contact faces representing maximum grip force. A secondary 15 N transverse load was applied at the fingertip to simulate workpiece slip loading.

8. Fatigue & Wear

Because the finger undergoes cyclic grip-and-release loading, fatigue endurance — not only static strength — governs service life. Aluminum 6061-T6 is non-ferrous and has no true endurance limit, so its fatigue strength is quoted at a finite life (~96 MPa at 5×10⁸ cycles). CFRP exhibits excellent fatigue resistance along the fibre direction, while SLS PA12's porosity makes it the weakest under cyclic load. At the contact face, the V-groove pads are treated as a replaceable consumable, so finger life is set by fatigue of the structural body rather than pad wear.

9. Physical Load Testing

Physical prototypes were manufactured in Al 6061-T6 (CNC milled) and PA12 Nylon (SLS printed). CFRP prototyping was not performed due to cost and fabrication time constraints at this project scale.

Each prototype was mounted in a benchtop load frame and loaded in 10 N increments. Fingertip displacement was recorded with a dial gauge and grip force with a calibrated load cell, with three repeat measurements per load step. The Al 6061-T6 prototype was tested non-destructively across the full 0–60 N range; the PA12 prototype, whose predicted factor of safety falls below 1.0 at the 50 N target, was loaded to failure to confirm its limit rather than carried through the complete range.

10. Business Case & LEAN

At a 10-unit batch, estimated cost per finger is ฿830 for Aluminum (material + CNC), ฿3,600 for CFRP (material + layup and finish), and ฿500 for PA12 (material + print). Aluminum is the cost-rational structural choice; CFRP's ~4× premium is justified only where weight is critical.

The modular architecture attacks the seven LEAN wastes — most directly waiting (tool-free finger swap under 2 minutes cuts changeover downtime), inventory (one actuator body plus a few finger SKUs replaces many dedicated grippers), and defects (V-groove self-centring reduces mis-picks). Estimated savings of ฿12,000 / year per robot (energy), ฿150,000 / month (defect reduction) and ฿25,000 / maintenance cycle (downtime) give an approximate payback of ~3.5 months. Figures are illustrative estimates pending plant-specific data; the full cost and LEAN tables are on the Development page.

11. Results & Discussion

Because the finger is a statically determinate cantilever, peak von Mises stress is governed by geometry and load rather than material — all three candidates see a near-identical ~95–102 MPa peak at the bayonet lug root. The materials separate decisively on the two metrics that do depend on material: stiffness (fingertip displacement) and factor of safety.

CFRP is the clear structural winner: its high modulus holds tip deflection to 0.32 mm, and its 600+ MPa strength yields a factor of safety of 6.3 — far above the ≥ 2.0 requirement. Al 6061-T6 meets the requirement comfortably (FoS 2.8) with a modest 0.41 mm deflection, making it the rational choice wherever in-house machinability and cost outweigh weight. PA12 Nylon fails the structural case: its predicted peak stress (102 MPa) exceeds its 48 MPa tensile strength (FoS 0.5), so the part yields well below the 50 N grip target, and its low modulus produces an unusable 17.8 mm elastic deflection.

For the physical prototypes, FEA-predicted displacement should be compared against the dial-gauge readings at each 10 N load step; agreement within ~10–15 % would validate the boundary-condition assumptions, while a larger gap would point to compliance at the bayonet interface not captured by the fixed-support idealization.

Key dimensions of comparison:

  • Structural: factor of safety at 50 N grip load
  • Mass: finger pair mass in grams
  • Stiffness: fingertip displacement per newton of grip force
  • Cost: estimated per-unit material and fabrication cost at 10-unit batch

12. Conclusion

CFRP delivers the best structural performance for the gripper finger application — lowest mass, highest stiffness, and the greatest factor of safety under the specified grip load. It is the recommended material for production units where weight is the primary constraint and per-unit cost is secondary.

Aluminum 6061-T6 is the recommended choice for cost-constrained production volumes. It meets all structural requirements, is easily machined in-house, and carries a substantially lower material cost than CFRP.

PA12 Nylon (SLS) is suitable for prototyping and geometry exploration only. Its factor of safety at 50 N is below the project requirement of 2.0 and its elastic modulus is too low for production-grade displacement control.
